The Brief

A manufacturer's data lives in five systems. Executives need one view.

Global manufacturers run on fragmented stacks: ERP for procurement, MES for production, WMS for inventory, CRM for sales, TMS for shipment. The cost is not technical — it's epistemic. Leaders cannot answer cross-functional questions in time to act on them.

Are we profitable on the customers we ship most to? Which suppliers cause our worst delays? Are inventory levels aligned with safety stock? This project simulates that problem for a Samsung-scale operation and builds the analytics layer that answers those questions in seconds, not days.

How It's Built

Bronze CSV in. Silver Parquet out. Pipeline-orchestrated.

Raw CSVs land in the Lakehouse /Files zone (Bronze). A Dataflow Gen2 cleans, types, and writes curated Parquet to /Tables (Silver). A pipeline guarantees the semantic model never refreshes against stale data.

What the Data Says

Finding 01

Profit margin is a healthy 27.44%, but the 24.71% imperfect-order rate signals meaningful revenue leakage from delays, defects, or short shipments — a quantifiable improvement target.

Finding 02

Inventory value of 160K against 3M shipment quantity highlights inventory turnover efficiency — a leading indicator for working-capital optimization.

Finding 03

Carrier-level delay tracking lets procurement renegotiate SLAs with underperforming carriers using evidence, not anecdote.

Finding 04

Finding 05

Channel-level revenue analysis enables sales leadership to prioritize the channels with the highest profit contribution, not just the highest top-line.

Design Decisions

Every choice has a counter-argument.

The interesting parts of a build are the trade-offs. Here's what I picked, why, and what I gave up.

Decision

Lakehouse over Warehouse

Why

Need both raw CSV (/Files) and structured tables (/Tables) in one place. Lower entry cost; ideal for a BI-first workload.

Trade-off

Warehouse offers richer T-SQL DML. Not needed for read-heavy reporting at this scale.

Decision

Dataflow Gen2 over Notebooks

Why

Low-code Power Query is faster to author and maintain for tabular CSV ingestion at 24K rows.

Trade-off

PySpark notebooks would scale further. Overkill at this volume.

Decision

Parquet output format

Why

Columnar compression dramatically speeds up DAX scans vs. row-based CSV.

Trade-off

Slightly slower writes than CSV. Acceptable in exchange for read performance.

Decision

Pipeline orchestration

Why

Guarantees Dataflow finishes before the semantic model refreshes. Eliminates stale-data risk.

Trade-off

Adds one orchestration layer to maintain. Worth it.

Decision

Star schema, not snowflake

Why

Simpler DAX, faster queries, easier for business users to read.

Trade-off

Some attribute redundancy in dimensions. Acceptable at this scale.

What I Learned

The build taught me more than the brief.

01

Orchestration > transformation

Building the Dataflow was straightforward. Getting the pipeline dependency order right was what made refresh reliable.

02

Grain discipline compounds

A clear per-fact-table grain definition upfront eliminated DAX double-counting bugs that would have hurt badly later.

03

Format choice is a lever

Switching curated output from CSV to Parquet measurably improved DAX scan times. A free win.

04

Pages earn the value

The Overview sells the story. The drill-down pages let executives answer the follow-up — which is what drives adoption.
